Activity 3 | Cash Flow Projection

Build a rolling 8-week cash flow projection for your practice business. Enter inflows and outflows using cash dates — when money actually moves, not when it is invoiced. Closing balances calculate automatically and carry forward. Negative weeks are flagged in red.

📋

Bring your Activity 1 figures here Your monthly revenue total (Activity 1 — Section 2) helps you estimate weekly inflows — divide by 4.3, then adjust for when customers actually pay. Your operating expenses (Activity 1 — Section 4) tell you which fixed costs fall in which week. Open Activity 1 in another tab as a reference.

What you're producing

An 8-week cash flow projection with opening balance, weekly inflows and outflows, net movement, and closing balance.

Cash dates used throughout — not invoice dates.

All negative weeks identified with a specific action proposed for each.

A reflection on the business's cash flow risk profile.

1. Set the context

Cash dates, not invoice dates

Enter the week cash actually arrives in your bank — not when you invoice. A $3,000 invoice with 14-day terms does not appear in this week's inflows. It appears two weeks from now.

📋 This is the cash in your business bank account at the start of Week 1. If estimating, a micro-firm typically holds 4–8 weeks of fixed costs as a buffer. Check your Activity 1 operating expenses total to calculate that range.

2. Weekly cash flow

Complete each week in sequence. Closing balance carries automatically to the next week's opening. Click any week header to expand or collapse it.

Estimating inflows? Divide your Activity 1 monthly revenue by 4.3 for a weekly average — then shift amounts to reflect when customers actually pay (after invoice terms). Estimating outflows? Identify which of your Activity 1 expense items fall in each week — rent is usually fortnightly or monthly; wages weekly; insurance and subscriptions may be quarterly.

3. Cash flow risk summary

Use the completed projection to identify the pattern of risk — not just individual negative weeks.

Is the risk concentrated in a particular period? Is there a structural cause — slow payments, a large fixed cost hitting in one week?

A change to payment terms or timing that reduces risk without affecting revenue.

4. Reflection

Two to three sentences. Focus on what the process surfaces.

Quality check

Have you used cash dates — not invoice dates — for all inflows?

Has every negative week been given a specific action — not just "chase payment"?

Does the risk summary identify a structural pattern, not just list individual weeks?
